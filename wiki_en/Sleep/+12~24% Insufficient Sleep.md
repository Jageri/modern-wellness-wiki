# Insufficient Sleep (<6 hours)

**Mortality Impact**: All-cause mortality +12–24%
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Multiple prospective cohort studies + meta-analyses)
**Actionability**: High

## Conclusion
Sleep 7–9 hours. 6 hours is the baseline; long-term below 6 hours significantly increases mortality.

## Evidence
- **Cappuccio et al. meta-analysis (2010)**: 16 prospective studies, 1.38M participants — short sleepers: all-cause mortality **+12%** (RR=1.12, 95%CI 1.06-1.18) [PMID: 20469800](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20469800/)
- **UK Biobank 407,500 participants (Tao 2021)**: ≤5h vs 7h — all-cause mortality **+25%** (HR=1.25, 95%CI 1.16-1.34); CVD mortality **+27%** (HR=1.27) [PMID: 33819843](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33819843/)
- **Cappuccio et al. meta-analysis (2011)**: Short sleep → CHD mortality **+48%** (RR=1.48, 95%CI 1.22-1.80); stroke risk **+15%** (RR=1.15) [PMID: 21300732](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21300732/)
- **Krittanawong et al. meta-analysis (2019)**: 19 studies, 817K individuals — short sleep (<7h) → CVD mortality **+19%** (RR=1.19, 95%CI 1.13-1.26); long sleep (>9h) → CVD mortality **+37%** (RR=1.37) [PMID: 29206050](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29206050/)
- **Yin et al. dose-response meta-analysis (2017)**: U-shaped curve, lowest risk at ~7h. Each 1h reduction below 7h: risk **+6%** (RR=1.06); each 1h increase above 7h: risk **+13%** (RR=1.13) [PMID: 28889101](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28889101/)
- Primary mechanisms: Decreased immune function (reduced NK cell activity), elevated inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6), metabolic disruption (decreased insulin sensitivity), accelerated cognitive decline
- Excessive sleep (>9h) is also associated with higher mortality (+30%, Cappuccio 2010), but causal direction is unclear — illness may cause longer sleep (reverse causality)

> **Note**: All evidence above comes from observational/prospective cohort studies, not randomized controlled trials. Self-reported sleep duration is subject to recall bias, and residual confounding (e.g., socioeconomic factors, underlying diseases) cannot be fully excluded. Studies are predominantly from high-income Western populations.

## Do
1. Fixed sleep-wake schedule, including weekends
2. Avoid screens 1 hour before bed
3. Keep the bedroom cool, quiet, and dark
4. Naps no more than 20 minutes, before 3 PM

## Don't
1. Don't believe "successful people only sleep 4 hours"—long-term damage
2. Don't use weekends for "revenge sleep"—disrupts circadian rhythm
3. Don't use alcohol to help sleep—causes early awakening and reduces sleep quality

## Medical Red Flags
- Unable to fall asleep for >30 minutes lasting more than 1 month
- Still sleepy during the day despite adequate sleep (rule out sleep apnea)
- Loud snoring + breathing pauses + daytime sleepiness (triad)
