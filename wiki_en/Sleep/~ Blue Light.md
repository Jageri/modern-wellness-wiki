# Blue Light Exposure

**Mortality Impact**: No direct evidence; primarily acts indirectly through sleep disruption
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐ (Clear indirect mechanisms, limited intervention efficacy)
**Actionability**: High

## Conclusion
Reducing screen use 2 hours before bed is the most effective core strategy. RCT evidence does not support significant improvement in objective sleep metrics from blue-light-blocking glasses; night mode effectiveness is uncertain. Reducing screen time is the fundamental solution. Adequate daytime natural light exposure supports nighttime sleep.

## Evidence
- Blue light (460–480 nm) suppresses pineal melatonin secretion via ipRGCs → SCN pathway, delaying sleep onset and shifting circadian phase [1][2]
- 2025 BBGs actigraphic meta-analysis (3 double-blind crossover RCTs, n=49): SOL MD −4.86 min (95% CI −20.23 to 10.52, p=0.54), TST MD 8.75 min (p=0.70), SE MD −0.61 (p=0.86) — all objective metrics non-significant [3]
- Cochrane 2023 systematic review (17 RCTs): "very low-certainty evidence" for blue-light filtering lenses on sleep quality; 3 of 6 RCTs reported improvement, 3 reported no difference [4]
- Psychological stimulation from screen content is also an important factor in sleep disruption; melatonin suppression and psychological arousal have additive effects [1]
- Sleep duration is associated with all-cause mortality (observational, n=1,382,999): short sleep RR 1.12 (95% CI 1.06–1.18), long sleep RR 1.30 (95% CI 1.22–1.38) [5]
- Blue light has positive daytime effects: increased alertness, cognitive performance, and reaction time; adequate daytime blue light exposure supports nighttime sleep [1]
- Melatonin suppression attenuates with age; light-circadian studies primarily in adults aged 20–40 [2]
- **Key limitations**: BBGs RCTs have small samples (max n=49), Cochrane review max follow-up only 5 weeks, actigraphy has measurement error

## Do
1. Reduce screen use 2 hours before bed — most effective, zero cost
2. Enable night mode on phone/computer — adjunctive, but effectiveness varies by device
3. No electronic devices in the bedroom
4. Adequate daytime outdoor activity and natural light exposure

## Don't
1. Don't rely solely on blue-light-blocking glasses — no significant improvement in objective sleep metrics (don't waste money)
2. Don't assume night mode means unlimited scrolling — psychological stimulation is also a problem
3. Don't wear blue-light-blocking glasses all day — daytime blue light has positive effects

## Medical Red Flags
- Persistent severe insomnia (>3 months) despite reducing screen time and blue light exposure → evaluate for sleep disorders
- Persistent vision decline
