# Religious Affiliation

**Mortality Impact**: Regular religious service attendance associated with -26~33% all-cause mortality (observational), with related life expectancy differences estimated at ~4-14 years (range depends on reference group and population)
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Multiple large prospective cohort studies, but all evidence is observational; causality unestablished)
**Actionability**: Varies by individual

## Conclusion
The association between religious service attendance and lower mortality does not primarily come from "what you believe," but is more likely related to "belonging"—the combined effect of community, ritual, purpose, and social support. Those without religious faith can obtain similar social connection benefits through other community activities.

## Evidence
- Li et al. (2016) US Nurses' Health Study (74,534 women, 16-year follow-up): Attending religious services once/week vs never → all-cause mortality HR 0.74 (95% CI 0.70-0.78) → **-26%**; more than once/week → HR 0.67 (95% CI 0.62-0.71) → **-33%**. Cardiovascular mortality -27% (HR 0.73), cancer mortality -21% (HR 0.79). Frequent attendees had ~5 years longer life expectancy.
- McCullough et al. (2000) Meta-analysis of 42 studies: Religious involvement significantly associated with survival (OR 1.29, 95% CI 1.20-1.39)
- Chen, VanderWeele et al. (2020) Three-cohort study (NHS, NHSII, GUTS): Attending services ≥1/week → all-cause mortality -26% (95% CI: 0.65-0.84). E-value analysis: an unmeasured confounder would need RR≥2.04 to fully explain the effect
- Blue Zones observation: All 5 longevity regions have deep religious/spiritual traditions, but this is popular observational evidence, not peer-reviewed research
- Proposed mechanisms (all correlational, causal chain unproven): Regular social contact + sense of purpose + stress-relief rituals + lifestyle regularization + social norms against smoking/drinking
- Non-religious alternatives: Volunteer activities, community clubs, meditation groups—the core is "regular, belonging-based social connection"

## Limitations & Counter-Evidence
- **Healthy user bias**: Regular attendees may have better baseline health and stronger social support
- **Reverse causation**: Declining health may reduce service attendance frequency
- **Population limits**: Li 2016 sample is US female nurses (predominantly white); most studies are from US Christian populations, generalizability to other religions/cultures uncertain
- **Observational evidence**: All evidence is observational; causal inference relies on statistical methods (e.g., E-value) to assess unmeasured confounding
- **Self-reported data**: Service attendance frequency is self-reported, subject to measurement error

## Do
1. For believers: Attend religious services regularly (≥1 time/week)
2. For non-believers: Join a regular community—book clubs, sports clubs, volunteer organizations
3. What matters isn't "what you believe," but "regular social connection with a sense of belonging"
4. When lonely: Having a pet is also an effective alternative (pet companionship associated with lower cardiovascular risk)
5. ⚠️ While the above suggestions are reasonable and harmless, direct RCT evidence that these practices reduce mortality is lacking
