# Social Isolation

**Mortality Impact**: All-cause mortality +32% (Nature Human Behaviour meta-analysis, observational evidence)
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (90 prospective cohort studies, 2 million+ adults; causal evidence pending confirmation)
**Actionability**: Medium

## Conclusion
Maintain at least one deep social relationship. A large body of observational research consistently links social isolation to increased risk of premature death (effect size approximately +28%–32%), but the existing evidence is predominantly observational — Mendelian randomization analyses suggest weak causal evidence, and the observed associations may be partially explained by socioeconomic factors, health behaviours, and biomarkers.

## Evidence
- **Wang et al. 2023** (PMID: 37337095): Meta-analysis of 90 prospective cohort studies (n=2,205,199). Social isolation → all-cause mortality RR 1.32 (95% CI 1.26–1.39); loneliness → RR 1.14 (95% CI 1.08–1.20). [Nature Human Behaviour](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41562-023-01617-6)
- **Zhou et al. 2024** (PMID: 38252435): In UK Biobank participants with obesity, social isolation ranked higher than loneliness, depression, anxiety, or lifestyle factors in predicting all-cause mortality. [JAMA Network Open](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2023.52824)
- **WHO 2025**: One in six people worldwide experience loneliness; loneliness accounts for approximately 871,000 deaths annually (~100 per hour). [WHO Commission on Social Connection](https://www.who.int/groups/commission-on-social-connection)
- **Holt-Lunstad et al. 2010** (PMID: 20668659): Meta-analysis of 148 studies (n=308,849). Strong social relationships → 50% higher likelihood of survival (OR 1.50, 95% CI 1.42–1.59). [PLoS Medicine](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.1000316)

## Limitations
- ⚠️ **Causality not established**: Ai et al. 2026 (PMID: 41872870) used Mendelian randomization to analyze social isolation and 59 health conditions, finding almost **no evidence supporting causality**. Observed associations were largely explained by proteomic signatures (36–49%), health behaviours (32–59%), and socioeconomic factors (22–42%).
- ⚠️ All primary evidence comes from **observational studies**; no RCTs exist. Reverse causation (poor health → social isolation) cannot be ruled out.
- ⚠️ Social isolation measurement varies significantly across studies (ranging from 3 questions to 18-item scales), with substantial between-study heterogeneity.
- ⚠️ The "comparable to smoking" argument from Holt-Lunstad 2010 is based on OR magnitude comparison, but the causal evidence for smoking (RCTs + mechanisms + dose-response) is far stronger than for social isolation.

## Do
1. At least 1 in-person social interaction per week (not online)
2. Maintain relationships where you can have deep conversations; quality > quantity
3. Join interest-based communities or volunteer activities
4. Those living alone: maintain regular contact with family and friends

## Don't
1. Don't treat "I don't need people" as independence—may be avoidance
2. Don't substitute social media "likes" for genuine connection
3. Don't ignore signals of "chronic loneliness"

## Medical Red Flags
- Long-term isolation + worsening mood + self-neglect
- Social avoidance lasting more than 1 month
- Loneliness accompanied by persistent insomnia or appetite changes
