# Sense of Purpose

**Mortality Impact**: Those with a clear sense of purpose have ~15–20% lower all-cause mortality and 2–7 years longer life expectancy
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Consistent across multiple prospective cohort studies)
**Actionability**: Medium (Requires sustained cultivation)

## Conclusion
Knowing "why you're alive" isn't just a philosophical question—it's a tangible longevity factor. Among the five Blue Zones, sense of purpose is the only characteristic shared by 100% of them.

## Evidence
- Canadian study (6,000 participants, 14-year follow-up): Those who could clearly articulate their sense of purpose had -15% death risk
- US National Institute on Aging: Those with a sense of purpose live 7 years longer
- Blue Zones: Okinawans call it "Ikigai," Nicoyans call it "plan de vida"—"why you wake up every morning"
- JAMA Psychiatry meta-analysis: Those with a sense of purpose had -17% all-cause mortality (especiallysignificantreduction in cardiovascular mortality)
- Mechanism: Sense of purpose → lower cortisol levels → reduced chronic inflammation → lower cardiovascular and neurodegenerative disease risk
- Relationship to depression: Sense of purpose is a protective factor against depression, but the two are notcompletelyequivalent

## Do
1. Find your "ikigai"—not a "goal," but "why you're alive"
2. Goals can be small: caring for family, gardening, teaching others something, volunteering
3. Especially important after retirement: Work itself provides purpose; losing it after retirement leads tosharphealth decline
4. Spend time each week reflecting: What activities make me lose track of time? What makes me feel valuable?
5. Social purpose is stronger than individual purpose (for others > for self)
