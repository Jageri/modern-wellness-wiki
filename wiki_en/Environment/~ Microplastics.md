# Microplastics

**Mortality Impact**: Association evidence suggests elevated cardiovascular risk (NEJM 2024 observational study; not causal confirmation)
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐ (Emerging research area; methodological controversies exist)
**Actionability**: Medium

## Conclusion
Reduce heating food in plastic containers and drinking bottled water. The strongest human evidence comes from a 2024 NEJM study: patients with microplastics detected in carotid artery plaque had approximately 4.5-fold higher risk of MI, stroke, or death compared to those without. However, note: the study shows association only, and the detection method (Py-GC-MS) faces growing scientific scrutiny—biological tissue components may be misidentified as plastic signals. The most reasonable current stance: prudently reduce exposure without panic.

## Evidence

### Human Studies
- **NEJM 2024 (PMID 38446676)**: 257 patients undergoing carotid endarterectomy; those with MNPs detected in plaque (58.4%) had significantly higher risk of the composite endpoint of MI, stroke, or all-cause death at 34-month follow-up (HR ≈ 4.5) [1]
- **Leslie et al. 2022 (PMID 35367073)**: First detection of microplastics in human blood; 77% (17/22) of donors tested positive for at least one polymer type (PET, PS, PE, PMMA, PP) [2]
- **Human thrombus detection (eBioMedicine/Lancet 2024)**: Microplastics detected in thrombi from multiple anatomical sites; higher concentrations associated with greater disease severity [3]
- **Scientific Reports 2024**: Blood microplastic levels associated with abnormal coagulation markers [4]

### Systematic Reviews
- **2024 Rapid Systematic Review (PMID 39692326)**: Microplastic exposure "suspected" to harm human reproductive, digestive, and respiratory health; suggested link to colon and lung cancer [5]
- **2025 Systematic Review**: Animal studies show microplastics can induce inflammation, oxidative stress, and cell damage, and can cross the blood-brain barrier [6]

### Bottled Water vs Tap Water
- Micro/nanoplastic content in bottled water is significantly higher than in tap water (some studies estimate hundreds of thousands of particles per liter)

## Methodological Controversies ⚠️ (2025–2026 Developments)
- **Py-GC-MS reliability questioned**: A 2025 study by Rauert et al. concluded this technique is "not currently suitable for identifying polyethylene or PVC due to persistent interferences"—biological components (e.g., lipids) may be misidentified as plastic signals [7]
- **Nature Medicine 2025 rebuttal letter**: Nine analytical chemists co-signed a letter arguing that Campen et al.'s brain microplastic study lacked sufficient contamination safeguards and validation, making reported brain concentrations unreliable [8]
- **2026 Guardian/Vox/Washington Post coverage**: Multiple analytical chemists believe the most widely cited microplastic human tissue detections may be false positives
- **Core issue**: "Detected" ≠ "harmful"; dose-response relationships, causal chains, and risk characterization at real-world concentrations remain lacking

## Do
1. Reduce heating food in plastic containers (especially microwave)
2. Prefer glass and stainless steel containers
3. Drink less bottled water; prioritize filtered tap water
4. Reduce use of plastic cutting boards (microplastic shedding during cutting)

## Don't
1. Don't put hot water in plastic bottles
2. Don't microwave plastic food containers
3. Don't panic excessively—current evidence is insufficient to prove acute harm; association ≠ causation

## Medical Red Flags
- Currently no acute symptoms requiring medical attention (chronic exposure issue)
- If you have a history of cardiovascular disease, discuss routine risk management (blood pressure, lipids, smoking cessation) with your physician
