# Noise Pollution

**Mortality Impact**: IHD risk +8%/10dB (long-term exposure), weaker association with CVD mortality
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐ (Epidemiological studies, GRADE low-to-moderate quality)
**Actionability**: Low

## Conclusion
Use earplugs in long-term noisy environments; soundproof the bedroom. Noise isn't just annoying—it genuinely harms your health, though direct RCT evidence for personal interventions is limited.

## Evidence
- Per 10 dB increase in traffic noise (Lden), IHD incidence increases ~8% (RR=1.08, 95% CI: 1.01–1.15) [PMID 24583674; WHO ENG 2018]
- Long-term noise exposure raises cortisol, blood pressure, oxidative stress, and inflammatory markers [PMID 39658622]
- Nighttime noise has stronger effects on blood pressure and vascular function than daytime noise (though chronic conditions like heart failure are more associated with daytime noise) [PMID 31922930; PMID 39658622]
- WHO estimates: Europe loses at least 1 million (range 1.0–1.6 million) healthy life years annually due to traffic noise [WHO 2011] — the vast majority from sleep disturbance and annoyance; IHD accounts for only ~61,000 DALYs
- Umbrella review (23 systematic reviews): high noise exposure associated with CVD risk +34% and CVD mortality +12% [PMID 37030015]
- 2025 meta-analysis: road noise per 10dB increases excess risk of IHD by 1.7%, MI by 2.9%, stroke by 2.5% [PMID 40444274]

## Limitations
- Most GRADE evidence rated low to very low quality [PMID 37030015]
- 2021 meta-analysis: road traffic noise and CVD mortality association not statistically significant (RR=1.01, 95% CI: 0.98–1.05) [PMID 33307398]
- Air pollution and noise share the same source (traffic), but current evidence suggests noise effects are independent of air pollution [PMID 37182833]; interaction effects require further study
- No direct RCT evidence that noise reduction interventions (earplugs/soundproofing) reduce CVD endpoints

## Do
1. Use noise-canceling earplugs in long-term noisy environments
2. Soundproof the bedroom (double-glazed windows, thick curtains)
3. Keep the bedroom quiet at night (WHO recommends <40 dB Lnight; <30 dB ideal)

## Don't
1. Don't use loud headphones to "cover" noise—double hearing damage
2. Don't ignore long-term low-intensity noise—chronic harm

## Medical Red Flags
- Tinnitus, hearing loss
- Persistent headaches after noise exposure
