# Socioeconomic Status

**Mortality Impact**: Low socioeconomic status (SES) is associated with 1.5–3× increased all-cause mortality
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (One of the strongest and most consistent health determinants globally)
**Actionability**: Low (difficult to change at the individual level, but awareness and strategies help)

## Conclusion
Socioeconomic status (income, education, occupation) is among the strongest predictors of mortality—its effect size is comparable to smoking and physical inactivity. This isn't simply "money buys longevity," but rather the combined effect of chronic stress from poverty, healthcare inaccessibility, clustering of behavioral risk factors, and adverse environments. Importantly, SES effects show substantial geographic heterogeneity (life expectancy for low-income individuals varies by up to 4.5 years across US regions), indicating the effect is partially modifiable.

## Evidence

### Core Studies
- **Chetty R et al. JAMA. 2016.** [PMID: 27063997](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27063997/): Analysis of 1.4 billion US tax records linking income to life expectancy. The richest 1% of men lived **14.6 years longer** on average than the poorest 1%; for women the gap was **10.1 years**. The gap widened between 2001–2014. Regions with longer life expectancy for the poor (e.g., New York City: 81.8 years) differed from the shortest (Gary, Indiana: 77.4 years) by 4.5 years.
- **Stringhini S et al. Lancet. 2017.** [PMID: 28159391](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28159391/): Meta-analysis of 48 cohorts and 1.7 million participants. Low SES (by occupational position) showed all-cause mortality HRs of **1.42 (men) to 2.31 (women)**—comparable to the WHO 25×25 target risk factors (smoking, physical inactivity, etc.). SES should be included in global NCD prevention strategies.
- **Whitehall studies** (British civil servant cohorts): Lowest employment grade vs highest → ~2–3× mortality rate increase. The gradient persists after adjusting for traditional risk factors, suggesting psychosocial pathways (sense of control, job strain) independent of material conditions.

### Education Effect
- **Balaj M et al. Lancet Public Health. 2024.** [PMID: 38278172](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38278172/): Global systematic review and meta-analysis. Each additional year of education was associated with a **2.9%** (95% CI: 2.8–3.0) reduction in mortality risk for adults aged **18–49**, but the effect diminished with age—only **0.8%** for those **≥70**. Having no education carries a mortality risk comparable to daily smoking.

### Mechanisms
- **Material pathway**: Income determines healthcare access, living environment (pollution exposure, neighborhood safety), nutrition quality
- **Psychosocial pathway**: Low SES → chronic stress (elevated cortisol, autonomic dysregulation) → cardiovascular disease; low sense of control, status anxiety
- **Behavioral pathway**: Higher prevalence of smoking, unhealthy diet, and physical inactivity in low-SES groups (Stringhini 2017 shows HR drops from 2.31 to ~1.6–1.8 after behavioral adjustment)
- **Structural pathway**: Education → health literacy → preventive behaviors → early diagnosis
- **Life-course pathway**: Childhood SES disadvantage accumulates into adulthood (birth weight, cognitive development, adult chronic disease risk)

### Counter-Evidence & Limitations
1. **Reverse causation**: Poor health → income/occupational downward drift (health selection hypothesis). Longitudinal adjustment for baseline health attenuates but does not eliminate the association.
2. **Behavioral confounding**: The SES–mortality association is attenuated by 30–50% after adjusting for health behaviors, but an independent effect remains.
3. **Geographic heterogeneity**: Life expectancy for individuals at the same SES level varies by up to 4.5 years across US regions (Chetty 2016), correlated more with smoking/obesity rates than with healthcare access.
4. **Indicator sensitivity**: Income, education, and occupation reflect different pathways to health; effect sizes differ by the SES indicator used.
5. **Observational evidence**: All evidence derives from observational studies; causality cannot be definitively established. No RCT exists (nor could exist) randomizing people to different income levels.

## Do (What you can control at the individual level)
1. **Health literacy**: Understanding basic health knowledge is the first step to bridging the healthcare access gap
2. **Regular checkups**: Early detection and treatment is the most cost-effective healthcare strategy
3. **Social networks**: Actively build supportive social relationships—social support is a health-protective factor independent of income
4. **Continuous learning**: The protective effect of education on health is independent of income—any form of learning is an investment
5. **Use free resources**: Exercise, sleep, and social connection are free; aerobic and strength training are associated with lower mortality independent of SES
6. **Environmental choices**: Within affordability, prioritize neighborhoods with good air quality and walkability (policy permitting)
