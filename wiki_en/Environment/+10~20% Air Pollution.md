# Air Pollution

**Mortality Impact**: Per 10 μg/m³ increase in PM2.5 → all-cause mortality +8% (global) / +11% (Asia-Pacific); cumulative effect in high-exposure areas can reach +10–20%
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Extensive cohort studies and meta-analyses; GRADE certainty rated "high")
**Actionability**: Low (individuals can hardly change the broader environment, but protective measures are effective)

## Conclusion
There is no safe threshold for long-term PM2.5 exposure—health effects persist below current WHO guideline levels. Wearing an N95 mask when AQI >150 and using a HEPA air purifier indoors are protective measures validated by RCTs. In the long term, living in areas with better air quality has a substantial impact on life expectancy.

## Evidence
- **Global meta-analysis** (Chen & Hoek, 2020, PMID 32703584, N=107 cohort studies): Per 10 μg/m³ PM2.5 increase, natural-cause mortality RR=1.08 (95%CI 1.06–1.09), GRADE certainty rated "high." Associations remained even below PM2.5 <10 μg/m³.
- **Asia-Pacific meta-analysis** (Oh et al., 2025, PMID 40662655, N=71 studies): Per 10 μg/m³ PM2.5 increase, all-cause mortality RR=1.11 (95%CI 1.05–1.17), CVD RR=1.13, lung cancer RR=1.12. Effect estimates in the Asia-Pacific region are higher than the global average.
- **WHO 2021 Global Air Quality Guidelines update** (2024, related PMID PMC11466858): 106 studies included; all exposure-outcome pairs showed positive and significant associations, further confirming the "no safe threshold" conclusion.
- **GBD 2021**: Air pollution ranks as the 4th leading global risk factor for premature mortality (after hypertension, smoking, and poor diet). PM2.5 remained the primary driver of pollution-attributable mortality in the U.S. as of 2021 (PMC12325274).
- **Huai River winter heating quasi-experiment** (Chen et al., 2013, PMID 23836630): Coal-based winter heating north of the Huai River resulted in TSP ~184 μg/m³ (+55%) higher, associated with ~5.5 years lower life expectancy (95%CI 0.8–10.2). Each additional 100 μg/m³ TSP reduced life expectancy by ~3.0 years.
- **Leading causes of death**: Ischemic heart disease, cerebrovascular disease (stroke), COPD, lower respiratory infections, lung cancer.

## Do
1. Monitor AQI; reduce outdoor activities when >150 (PM2.5 ≈55 μg/m³, US EPA "unhealthy" category)
2. Wear an N95/KN95 mask on heavily polluted days (RCT evidence: improved FEV1 by +2–3%, reduced respiratory oxidative stress and systemic inflammation; Jiang et al., 2021, PMID 34265631)
3. Use an air purifier with a HEPA filter indoors
4. If possible, choose to live in areas with better air quality (long-term exposure differences can translate to several years of life expectancy)

## Don't
1. Don't exercise outdoors on heavily polluted days—acute cardiovascular risk from PM2.5 is compounded by exercise-induced effects, with clear net harm
2. Don't ignore indoor air pollution (cooking fumes, formaldehyde from renovations, solid fuel heating)
3. Don't substitute surgical masks for N95—surgical masks filter only ~30–50% of PM2.5, vs. ≥95% for N95
4. Don't assume AQI <150 is safe—health effects are detectable at PM2.5 levels as low as <5 μg/m³; there is no clearly established safe threshold

## ⚠️ Proximate Mechanisms
Air pollution increases mortality risk through the following proximate pathways:
- **PM2.5 enters lungs and bloodstream** → oxidative stress → endothelial dysfunction → accelerated atherosclerosis → ischemic heart disease, stroke
- **Chronic pulmonary inflammation** → COPD progression, lung carcinogenesis
- **Systemic inflammation** → insulin resistance → diabetes (GBD 2021 has included diabetes as a PM2.5-attributable outcome)
- **Epigenetic alterations** → abnormal DNA methylation → long-term health damage

## Controversies & Limitations
- **Causality vs. association**: Most evidence comes from observational cohort studies. Despite quasi-experimental designs (Huai River policy) and RCTs (mask interventions), direct causal inference at the individual level remains limited.
- **Confounding**: Air pollution correlates with income, healthcare access, and occupational exposures. Adjustment for these factors varies across meta-analyses.
- **Shape of the dose-response curve**: Some studies suggest a supra-linear relationship (steeper at low doses), while the non-linear Integrated Exposure-Response (IER) model used by GBD flattens at high exposure levels—these two assumptions have different policy implications.
- **Mask side effects**: Prolonged N95 wear can cause discomfort from increased breathing resistance; caution is warranted in those with impaired cardiac function.

## Medical Red Flags
- Persistent cough, chest tightness after smog exposure
- Respiratory symptoms that don't resolve after air quality improves
