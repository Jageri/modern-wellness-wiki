# Thermoregulation (Cold/Heat Exposure)

**Mortality Impact**: Non-optimal temperature accounts for ~7.7% of global mortality; cold (7.3%) far exceeds heat (0.4%)
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Strong evidence for extreme temperature harm; RCT and observational evidence for sauna and cold adaptation benefits)
**Actionability**: Medium

## Conclusion
Avoid extreme temperature exposure. Moderate cold/heat exposure may activate adaptive mechanisms via hormesis, but dose and individual variation matter. Cold is a far larger global mortality risk than heat waves—a fact at odds with public perception.

## Evidence

### Mortality Burden of Extreme Temperatures
- **Cold dominates**: Gasparrini et al. (2015, Lancet, PMID: 26003380) analyzed 74,225,200 deaths across 13 countries. Non-optimal temperature accounted for 7.71% of mortality, with cold (7.29%) far exceeding heat (0.42%). Most attributable deaths came from moderate cold rather than extreme temperatures.
- **Heat waves**: Zhao et al. (2024, PLOS Medicine) estimated ~153,078 excess heatwave-related deaths/year globally (1990–2019), accounting for 0.94% of warm-season deaths. Heat stroke (core temperature >40°C) carries extremely high mortality.
- **Extreme cold**: Increases cardiovascular events (blood pressure surge, increased blood viscosity) and respiratory disease risk.

### Sauna (Heat Hormesis)
- **Laukkanen et al. 2015** (JAMA Intern Med, PMID: 25705824): 2,315 Finnish men aged 42–60, median follow-up 20.7 years. 4–7 vs 1 session/week: SCD HR = 0.37 (95%CI 0.18–0.75); significant reductions for fatal CHD, fatal CVD, and all-cause mortality. Sessions lasting >19 min superior to <11 min.
- **Laukkanen et al. 2018** (BMC Med, PMID: 30486813): Extended to women (n=1,688, 51.4% female), median follow-up 15 years. 4–7 vs 1 session/week: CVD mortality HR = 0.23 (95%CI 0.08–0.65), fully adjusted. Linear dose-response for both frequency and duration.
- **2023 follow-up** (PMID: 37248758): Frequent sauna bathing attenuated the elevated CVD mortality risk associated with high systolic blood pressure.

### Cold Exposure & Brown Adipose Tissue (BAT)
- **Meta-analysis** (2022, PMC9273773): 10 RCTs pooled. Acute cold exposure (16–19°C vs 24°C room temperature) increased energy expenditure by ~188 kcal/day; BAT volume and activity also increased significantly.
- **Romu et al. RCT** (2016, PMID: 27173471): 6 weeks of 1h/day cold exposure vs. avoiding cold. Cold group showed significantly increased supraclavicular BAT volume (p = 0.049) and a trend toward increased metabolic rate (p = 0.052).

### Contrary Evidence & Controversies
- **Sauna causality questioned**: Kivimäki et al. (2015, PMID: 26436738) and Epstein et al. (2015, PMID: 26436739) published comments in JAMA Intern Med arguing the sauna-mortality association may be noncausal (healthy user bias: those able to sauna frequently may be healthier at baseline).
- **Cold exposure evidence limitations**: BAT activation studies are mostly small (n < 30), short-term (weeks), and lack long-term hard endpoints (mortality / CVD events).

## Mechanisms
- **Sauna / Heat exposure**: Induces heat shock proteins (HSPs), improves endothelial function, lowers blood pressure, increases heart rate variability, mimics hemodynamic effects of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise
- **Cold exposure**: Sympathetic nervous system releases norepinephrine → brown adipose tissue β-adrenergic receptors → UCP1 uncoupling thermogenesis → increased energy expenditure; repeated exposure increases BAT volume and activity

## Do
1. Sauna: 80–100°C dry sauna, 4–7 sessions/week, ≥15 min/session (start gradually: shorter and less frequent)
2. Cold exposure: Consider 1h/day at 16–19°C (start brief, prioritize safety)
3. Hot weather: Hydrate, replenish electrolytes, avoid midday sun
4. Cold weather: Keep warm (especially head and core), attend to cardiovascular protection

## Don't
1. Don't push through extreme temperatures
2. Don't attempt sudden extreme cold exposure (cardiovascular risk, may trigger coronary vasospasm)
3. Don't push through heat stroke (core temp >40°C is a medical emergency—cool and seek care immediately)
4. Consult a physician before starting sauna or cold exposure if you have cardiovascular disease

## Medical Red Flags
- Heat stroke (core temp >40°C, confusion, hot dry skin without sweating)
- Persistent shivering, palpitations, confusion after cold exposure
- Chest pain, difficulty breathing after extreme temperature exposure
