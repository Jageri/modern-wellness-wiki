# Dry Sauna

**Mortality Impact**: Cardiovascular mortality approximately -50% (4–7 sessions/week), all-cause mortality -40%
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Large prospective cohort studies)
**Actionability**: Medium (Requires sauna facilities)

## Conclusion
The cardiovascular protection from sauna comes from the Finnish Kuopio Ischemic Heart Disease (KIHD) Risk Factor Study — which followed 2,315 middle-aged men for a median of 20.7 years, finding that frequent sauna use was associated with significantly reduced cardiovascular and all-cause mortality. A subsequent 2018 study extended these findings to women. The same cohort also found that frequent sauna use was associated with a 66% reduction in dementia risk.

⚠️ **Important limitations**: All evidence comes from observational studies in Finnish populations with healthy-user bias; no randomized controlled trials confirm causality.

## Evidence
- **Finnish KIHD Study** (2,315 men, median 20.7-year follow-up): 4–7 sauna sessions/week vs 1 session → all-cause mortality approximately -40%, cardiovascular mortality approximately -50%; SCD HR 0.37 (95% CI 0.18–0.75); sauna duration >19 min vs <11 min, SCD HR 0.48 (95% CI 0.31–0.75)
- **BMC Medicine 2018 extension study** (1,688 participants, including women): confirmed strong inverse associations between sauna frequency/duration and CVD mortality, and adding sauna frequency to risk prediction models improved long-term prediction
- **Dementia/Alzheimer's** (same KIHD cohort): 4–7 sessions/week → 66% lower dementia risk, 65% lower Alzheimer's risk
- **Core mechanism**: heat exposure → heart rate rises to 100–150 bpm (producing cardiovascular stress similar to moderate-intensity exercise) → improved vascular endothelial function → blood pressure reduction (acute 5–10 mmHg) → decreased inflammatory markers (CRP) → heat shock protein activation
- Peter Attia's recommendation: 4 times per week, 20 minutes each, above 82°C (179°F) (widely cited from his podcast; verify exact source if needed)
- Infrared sauna effects may be similar, but evidence is less robust than traditional dry sauna (KIHD used traditional Finnish sauna at 80–90°C; infrared saunas operate at 45–60°C with different mechanisms)
- Note: Those with severe cardiovascular disease should consult their doctor

## Do
1. 3–4 times per week, 15–20 minutes each
2. Temperature above 80°C for optimal effect (optimal range 80–90°C)
3. Hydrate thoroughly before and after sauna
4. Never sauna after alcohol consumption (~30% of sauna-related cardiac events involve alcohol, significantly increasing sudden death risk)
5. If no sauna access, hot baths are an alternative (Japanese cohort study confirms association with reduced CVD risk; slightly weaker effect but supported by data)
