# VO2 Max

**Mortality Impact**: One of the strongest modifiable predictors of mortality; low VO2 max individuals have ~5x the mortality risk of high VO2 max individuals
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Extensive prospective studies)
**Actionability**: High (can be significantly improved through training)

## Conclusion
VO2 max (maximal oxygen uptake) is the gold standard for measuring cardiorespiratory fitness and one of the strongest predictors of all-cause mortality. It has stronger predictive power than traditional risk factors such as blood pressure, cholesterol, and smoking. Good news: it can be significantly improved through training.

## Evidence
- Mandsager study (122,007 patients undergoing exercise treadmill testing): Lowest fitness group vs elite had HR 5.04 (CI 4.10-6.20), i.e., ~5x the mortality risk [1]
- In the same study, low VO2 max mortality risk ratio (HR 5.04) far exceeded smoking (HR 1.41), diabetes (HR 1.40), and coronary heart disease (HR 1.29) [1]
- Kodama meta-analysis: CRF has a quantitative inverse association with all-cause mortality and cardiovascular events [2]
- VO2 max naturally declines ~10% per decade (sedentary individuals), possibly accelerating to ~15% after age 50
- Even starting training in middle age can partially reverse decline
- **Note**: These are observational studies; causal relationship not established. Mandsager study is based on patients undergoing exercise treadmill testing (symptomatic or at-risk), not the general population

## Do
1. **Measure your baseline**: Estimate VO2 max through exercise testing or smartwatch
2. **Zone 2 training**: 3–4 times per week, 45 minutes each (heart rate 70–85% of max heart rate)
3. **High-intensity interval training (HIIT)**: 1–2 times per week, 4 minutes high intensity + 4 minutes recovery, repeat 4–6 sets
4. **Targets**: Men >40 mL/kg/min, Women >35 mL/kg/min (for the 40s age group)
5. **Priority**: Improving VO2 max yields greater benefits than reducing body fat percentage
