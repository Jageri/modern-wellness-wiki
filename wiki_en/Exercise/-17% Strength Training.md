# Strength Training

**Mortality Impact**: All-cause mortality approximately -15–20% (regular strength trainers), independently additive with aerobic exercise
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Multiple prospective cohort studies)
**Actionability**: High

## Conclusion
The mortality protection from strength training is independent of aerobic exercise, and the two have additive effects when combined. Muscle mass is a strong predictor of survival in older adults, and "sarcopenia" is an independent mortality risk factor.

## Evidence
- US NHANES cohort: Those who strength trained ≥2 times per week had -17% all-cause mortality and -21% cancer mortality
- Australian study (80,306 participants): Strength training associated with -15% all-cause mortality; effect doubled when combined with aerobic exercise
- Sarcopenia patients: ~20–30% mortality within 1 year after fall-related fractures (hip fractures are particularly fatal)
- Core mechanisms: Maintains insulin sensitivity, bone density, basal metabolic rate, and functional independence
- Key indicators: Grip strength (strong predictor of all-cause mortality), leg strength, gait speed

## Do
1. Full-body strength training 2–3 times per week
2. Focus on compound movements: squats, deadlifts, bench press, rows, overhead press
3. Progressive overload: gradually increase weight/reps/sets
4. For those 60+: prioritize grip strength, single-leg stand, ability to rise from the floor
5. Protein intake: 1.6–2.2 g/kg/day (strength trainers need more)
6. Don't neglect eccentric training (the lowering phase) and hip hinge movements
