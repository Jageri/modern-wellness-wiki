# Strength Training

**Mortality Impact**: All-cause mortality approximately -15% (meta-analysis); independently additive with aerobic exercise
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Multiple prospective cohort studies + meta-analysis)
**Actionability**: High

## Conclusion
The mortality protection from strength training is independent of aerobic exercise, and the two combined have the greatest effect. Muscle mass is a strong predictor of survival in older adults; sarcopenia is an independent mortality risk.

## Evidence
- Shailendra 2022 meta-analysis (10 studies): Any resistance training vs none, all-cause mortality -15% (RR 0.85), CVD mortality -19%, cancer mortality -14%
- Optimal dose: ~60 minutes/week of resistance training, maximum risk reduction -27% (RR 0.74); diminishing returns at higher volumes
- Sarcopenia patients: 20-30% mortality rate within 1 year of fall-related fractures (hip fractures especially lethal)
- Core mechanisms: Maintains insulin sensitivity, bone density, basal metabolic rate, functional independence
- Key indicators: Grip strength (strong all-cause mortality predictor), leg strength, gait speed

## Do
1. 2-3 full-body strength training sessions per week
2. Focus on compound movements: squats, deadlifts, bench press, rows, overhead press
3. Progressive overload: gradually increase weight/reps/sets
4. For 60+ years: focus on grip strength, single-leg standing, floor-to-stand ability
5. Protein intake: 1.6-2.2 g/kg/day (strength trainers need more)
6. Don't neglect eccentric training (lowering phase) and hip-hinge movements

## References
- Shailendra P et al. Resistance Training and Mortality Risk: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis. Am J Prev Med. 2022.
