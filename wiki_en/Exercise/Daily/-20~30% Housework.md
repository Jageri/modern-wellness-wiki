# Housework

**Mortality Impact**: Productive activities such as housework are associated with improved survival in older adults (observational evidence)
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐ (Observational studies; direct mortality quantification is limited)
**Actionability**: Very High

## Conclusion
Housework combines physical activity, cognitive planning, and sense of purpose—all associated with better health outcomes in older adults. It should not be viewed as a substitute for dedicated exercise, but is an underappreciated form of daily physical activity.

## Evidence
- Glass 1999 BMJ prospective cohort study (2,761 US adults aged ≥65, 13-year follow-up): Productive activities including housework, shopping, and cooking were independently associated with improved survival [1]
- Mechanism: Housework combines physical activity (scrubbing, sweeping, carrying) + cognitive planning + sense of purpose
- **Important caveat**: Direct quantitative mortality data specifically for housework (e.g., -20–30%) lack high-quality research support. Existing evidence is primarily associational, and the effect of housework may be confounded by overall activity levels
- Blue Zones: Longevity hotspots feature continuous engagement in housework, gardening, and other daily physical activities—but this is qualitative observation, not quantitative evidence [2]

## Do
1. Don't outsource all housework—doing it yourself is free daily activity
2. Older adults in particular should maintain housework habits
3. Gardening has similar effects
4. Cooking counts too: meal preparation involves standing, walking, and fine motor movements
5. Do not view housework as a substitute for dedicated exercise—maintain a separate exercise routine

## References
