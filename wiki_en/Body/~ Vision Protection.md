# Vision Protection

**Mortality Impact**: No direct mortality evidence, but affects quality of life
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐ (Strong evidence for myopia prevention)
**Actionability**: High

## Conclusion
Outdoor time is the most effective prevention strategy for myopia in children and adolescents (highest vs. lowest outdoor time OR=0.53) [3]. Myopia isn't just about "not seeing clearly" — high myopia (>−6.00 D) is significantly associated with increased risk of several blinding eye diseases. The 20-20-20 rule has limited evidence but is harmless.

## Evidence
- High myopia (>−6.00 D) is significantly associated with increased risks of retinal detachment (OR 12.62, 95% CI 6.65–23.94), open-angle glaucoma (OR 2.92), and posterior subcapsular cataract (OR 4.55) [1]
- Outdoor time is the strongest protective factor against myopia in children: each additional hour per day reduces myopia risk by ~13%; highest vs. lowest outdoor time OR=0.53 [2][3]
- ⚠️ Important caveat: outdoor time prevents myopia **onset**, but does **not** slow progression in children who are already myopic (Li 2024 meta-analysis) [3]
- The 20-20-20 rule (every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds) lacks high-quality clinical evidence — a 2023 study noted its effectiveness evidence "does not appear to be well established," but it is widely recommended as a low-cost, harmless behavioral suggestion [4]
- Global myopia rates continue to rise: Holden et al. systematic review projects ~49.8% of the world population (4.758 billion people) will be myopic by 2050 [5]
- Blue-light filtering glasses: Cochrane systematic review (17 RCTs) concluded they probably make no difference to eye strain or sleep quality [6]

## Do
1. **Children and adolescents**: 1–2 hours of outdoor activity daily (strongest evidence for myopia prevention)
2. Take breaks to look into the distance after 30–40 minutes of near work
3. Maintain appropriate distance between screen and eyes
4. Regular eye exams (annual fundus exam for those with high myopia)

## Don't
1. Don't look at your phone in the dark (may worsen eye strain)
2. Don't ignore rapid changes in myopia prescription
3. Don't waste money on blue-light-blocking glasses — RCT evidence does not support their effectiveness [6]

## Medical Red Flags
- Sudden vision loss, worsening floaters, visual field defects
- Regular fundus exams for those with high myopia
