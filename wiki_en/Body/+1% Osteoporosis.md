# Osteoporosis

**Mortality Impact**: ~20% mortality within 1 year of hip fracture
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Osteoporosis clinical consensus)
**Actionability**: High

## Conclusion
Dietary calcium + vitamin D + weight-bearing exercise form the basic strategy for osteoporosis prevention. However, note: the USPSTF (2024) found with moderate certainty that vitamin D with or without calcium provides no net benefit for primary fracture prevention in community-dwelling older adults without osteoporosis. Those already diagnosed should still ensure adequate intake.

## Evidence
- Approximately 8.9 million osteoporotic fractures occur globally each year, averaging one every 3 seconds (IOF, 2019)
- ~20% mortality within 1 year of hip fracture; this rate remained stable from 1981–2012 (Mundi et al., 2014)
- ~1/3 of women and 1/5 of men over 50 will experience an osteoporotic fracture in their remaining lifetime (IOF / Kanis et al., Osteoporos Int, 2000)
- Peak bone mass timing varies by skeletal site: lumbar spine peaks at 33–40 years, total hip at 16–19 years (NOF Position Statement, PMID: 26856587)
- USPSTF 2024 draft: vitamin D with or without calcium shows no net benefit for primary fracture prevention in community-dwelling older adults (moderate certainty)

## Do
1. Daily calcium intake 1000–1200mg (milk, tofu, dark leafy greens)—prioritize food sources; calcium supplements may increase cardiovascular risk (PMID: 31625814)
2. Vitamin D: 15–20 minutes of sun exposure daily; supplement 800–1000 IU/day if deficient
3. Weight-bearing exercise (walking, running, strength training)—multiple meta-analyses confirm significant BMD improvements from exercise
4. Regular DXA bone density testing after age 50; T-score <-2.5 indicates osteoporosis

## Don't
1. Don't just take calcium supplements without exercise—bones need mechanical stimulation to retain calcium
2. Don't assume "calcium = bone soup"—bone soup has extremely low calcium content
3. Don't neglect vitamin D—in severe deficiency, intestinal calcium absorption efficiency can drop by >75% (MDPI Nutrients, 2022), but the effect is much smaller in mild insufficiency
4. Don't take high-dose calcium supplements indiscriminately—supplemental calcium (not dietary) may be associated with increased cardiovascular event risk; prioritize dietary sources

## Medical Red Flags
- Height loss >3cm, worsening kyphosis (may indicate vertebral compression fractures)
- Fracture from minor trauma (fragility fracture)
- Bone density T-score <-2.5
