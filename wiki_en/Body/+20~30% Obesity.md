# Obesity (BMI >30)

**Mortality Impact**: All-cause mortality +20–44% (Class I obesity); per 5-unit BMI increase above 25, HR ≈1.21–1.52 (age-dependent)
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Consistent large cohorts + meta-analyses; causality pending RCT confirmation)
**Actionability**: High

## Conclusion
Caloric deficit (300–500 kcal/day) + strength training to preserve muscle. Focus on waist circumference and body fat percentage, not weight. No crash dieting.

## Evidence
- BMI 30–34.9: all-cause mortality HR 1.44 (95%CI 1.38–1.50); BMI ≥40: HR 2.51 (Berrington de Gonzalez et al. NEJM. 2010, n=1.46M, PMID: 21121834)
- Lancet meta-analysis of 239 prospective studies: per 5 kg/m² higher BMI above 25, HR 1.52 (age 35–49) → 1.21 (age 70–89) (Di Angelantonio et al. 2016, PMID: 27423262)
- Waist circumference/waist-to-hip ratio predict cardiovascular mortality better than BMI alone (JAMA Netw Open. 2023;6(10):e2339856, UK Biobank n=387,672)
- >80% of lost weight regained within 5 years post-weight loss (Hall & Kahan, Med Clin North Am. 2018, PMC5764193)
- Each major weight loss cycle reduces resting energy expenditure by 200–500 kcal/d via metabolic adaptation (Fothergill et al. Obesity. 2016, PMID: 27136388)
- Strength training + moderate caloric deficit preserves lean mass better than diet alone (Weiss et al. J Appl Physiol. 2017, PMID: 28183894)

## Counter-Evidence
- **Obesity paradox**: In older adults with existing chronic disease, overweight/mild obesity may associate with lower short-term mortality vs. normal weight (Nutrients 2023 systematic review, 58 studies, PMC10096985)
- However, in healthy older adults without pre-existing disease, 16/20 studies found NO obesity paradox; Class I obesity still increased mortality
- U-shaped BMI-mortality curve: BMI <20 also increases mortality (potentially confounded by undiagnosed disease-driven weight loss)

## Do
1. Daily caloric deficit of 300–500 kcal (no more)
2. Protein intake 1.2–1.6 g/kg body weight (preserve muscle)
3. Strength training 2–3× per week
4. Track waist circumference (men <90 cm, women <85 cm), not just weight

## Don't
1. No crash dieting (<1,200 kcal/day) — metabolic adaptation ensures harder rebound
2. Don't rely on weight alone — muscle is denser than fat; weight may stay stable or increase while body composition improves
3. Don't trust non-prescription "diet pills" — most lack long-term safety and efficacy data

## Population Notes
- Meta-analyses primarily from high-income populations (Europe, North America, East Asia); data from South Asia and Africa are limited
- Older adults (70+): BMI-mortality association weakens; weight-loss decisions require individualized assessment
- Asian populations: higher body fat % at same BMI; waist circumference thresholds should be lower (men <85 cm, women <80 cm may be more appropriate)

## Medical Red Flags
- Normal BMI but extreme fear of weight gain (rule out body dysmorphic disorder)
- Rapid unexplained weight gain (rule out endocrine disorders: hypothyroidism, Cushing's syndrome)
- Eating disorder symptoms following weight loss
