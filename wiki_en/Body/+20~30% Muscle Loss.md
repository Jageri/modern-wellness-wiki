# Muscle Loss / Sarcopenia

**Mortality Impact**: Sarcopenia patients have significantly elevated mortality after falls and fractures
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Geriatric medicine consensus)
**Actionability**: High

## Conclusion
1.0–1.2 g/kg protein per day (distributed across three meals) + strength training 2–3 times per week. Muscle is the best "insurance."

## Evidence
- 3–8% muscle mass loss per decade after age 30, accelerating after 60
- Sarcopenic individuals have ~2× increased fall risk (prospective studies OR 1.89, 95% CI 1.33–2.68) [PMID: 30993881]
- ~20% mortality within 1 year of hip fracture [Mundi et al., 1981–2012 meta-analysis]
- Grip strength is an independent predictor of all-cause mortality (42 studies, 3 million participants, HR 1.41) [PMID: 28549705]
- ⚠️ Contrary evidence: Vitamin D monotherapy shows no significant improvement in sarcopenia indices (grip strength, muscle mass, physical performance) [PMID: 35261183]
- Protein intake combined with exercise is the optimal strategy for maintaining muscle function [ESPEN, PMID: 24814383]

## Do
1. Adequate daily protein intake (total 1.0–1.2 g/kg, evenly distributed across three meals) [ESPEN]
2. Strength training 2–3 times per week (squats, deadlifts, and other compound movements; progressive resistance training recommended) [ESPEN]
3. Higher protein needs for those 65+ with malnutrition or illness risk (1.2–1.5 g/kg/day) [ESPEN]
4. Ensure adequate sun exposure to maintain vitamin D levels (but vitamin D supplementation alone has insufficient evidence for sarcopenia prevention)

## Don't
1. Don't assume "getting thinner is just part of aging"—muscle loss ≠ normal aging
2. Don't do only aerobic exercise without strength training—aerobic exercise cannot prevent muscle loss
3. Don't fear "bulking up"—normal training won't cause that
4. Don't rely on vitamin D supplements to prevent sarcopenia—current evidence does not support monotherapy effectiveness

## Medical Red Flags
- Noticeable grip strength decline (can't open bottle caps)
- Difficulty standing up, significantly slower walking speed
- Unexplained weight loss
