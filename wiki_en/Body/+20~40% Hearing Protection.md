# Hearing Protection

**Mortality Impact**: Hearing loss associated with ~20% increased all-cause mortality (HR 1.21, 95% CI 1.13–1.31), and 2–5× increased cognitive decline risk (depending on severity)
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Large prospective studies + one RCT, but primary endpoint negative)
**Actionability**: High

## Conclusion
Hearing loss isn't just about "not hearing clearly"—it is significantly associated with increased all-cause mortality and cognitive decline risk, potentially through social isolation, increased cognitive load, and elevated fall risk. Hearing aids may slow cognitive decline in high-risk populations, but a universal protective effect against cognitive decline has not been confirmed by RCTs.

## Evidence

### Hearing Loss and Dementia
- **Lancet Standing Commission 2024 Report**: Hearing loss is one of the largest modifiable risk factors for dementia (1 of 14 risk factors, population attributable fraction ~8%)
- **Lin FR et al. (2011, Arch Neurol, n=639)**: Per 10 dB of hearing loss, all-cause dementia risk increased by 27% (HR 1.27, 1.06–1.50). Moderate HL: HR 3.00 (1.43–6.30); severe HL: HR 4.94 (1.09–22.40)

### Hearing Loss and Mortality
- **Jia J et al. 2025 Meta-analysis (PLoS One)**: 36 cohort studies, 6.4 million participants. Hearing loss associated with all-cause mortality (HR 1.21, 1.13–1.31), cardiovascular mortality (HR 1.22, 1.12–1.33), and cancer mortality (HR 1.11, 1.02–1.22). Audiometrically-measured HL showed a stronger effect (HR 1.28).

### Hearing Aid Intervention
- **ACHIEVE Trial (Lancet 2023, 3-year RCT, ages 70–84)**: In the primary analysis (n=977), the hearing intervention group showed **no significant difference** in cognitive decline versus control. However, in a prespecified high-risk subgroup (ARIC participants, n=238), cognitive decline was slowed by 48%.
- **UK Biobank Cohort (Jiang F et al., Lancet Public Health 2023, n≈437,000)**: Hearing aid use associated with reduced all-cause dementia risk (HR ≈0.58), but this is observational with potential healthy-user bias.
- **Contrary Evidence (PMID 41534012, 2026)**: In adults ≥70 with moderate hearing impairment, hearing aid use did not improve overall cognitive scores (Class III evidence).

### Mechanism
- Hypothesized pathway: Auditory deprivation → social withdrawal → reduced cognitive stimulation → accelerated brain atrophy. Each link has supporting epidemiological evidence, but the complete causal chain has not been confirmed by RCTs. Hearing loss may also be an early marker of neurodegeneration.

### Noise Exposure
- Long-term or repeated exposure to ≥85 dBA can cause permanent hearing damage (NIDCD/WHO consensus).
- Approximately 14% of US adults aged ≥50 who need hearing aids use them; usage drops to <4% for ages 50–59 (NIDCD).

## ⚠️ Important Caveats
- **Hearing aids ≠ guaranteed brain protection**: ACHIEVE is the only large-scale RCT, and its primary analysis was **negative**. Hearing aids may primarily benefit high-risk subgroups.
- **Observational ≠ causal**: Hearing aid users tend to have higher socioeconomic status and greater health awareness (healthy-user bias); benefits seen in observational studies may be overestimated.
- **Hearing loss may be a consequence, not a cause**: Some evidence suggests hearing decline may be an early marker of neurodegeneration rather than an independently modifiable risk factor. This question remains unresolved.

## Do
1. Protect hearing: Headphone volume not exceeding 60% of max; continuous use not exceeding 60 minutes
2. Use earplugs or noise-canceling headphones in noisy environments
3. Regular hearing checks after age 50
4. For those with hearing loss: Evaluate hearing aid suitability early (earlier intervention = better brain adaptation)
5. Don't think "I can still hear so it's fine"—hearing loss is gradual; by the time it's obvious, it's already advanced
