# Tooth Brushing

**Mortality Impact**: Periodontal disease associated with increased all-cause mortality (RR 1.31, 95%CI 1.07–1.61, large meta-analysis)
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Consistent across multiple prospective cohort studies, but RCT causal evidence is lacking)
**Actionability**: Very High

## Conclusion
Oral hygiene (brushing, flossing, regular dental cleaning) is associated with lower cardiovascular disease risk and all-cause mortality. The proposed mechanism involves chronic oral inflammation → elevated systemic inflammatory markers → vascular endothelial damage → atherosclerosis. Note: All existing evidence is observational; causality has not been confirmed by randomized controlled trials.

## Evidence

### Supporting Evidence
- **Toothbrushing and CVD risk** (de Oliveira et al. BMJ 2010, PMID 20508025): Scottish Health Survey. Brushing <1/day vs ≥2/day → fatal CVD HR 2.40 (1.50–4.00), CVD events HR ~1.7. Follow-up ~8.1 years.
- **Periodontal disease and all-cause mortality** (Guo et al. PLOS ONE 2023): Meta-analysis of 39 cohort studies, 4.39 million participants → PD→all-cause mortality RR 1.31 (1.07–1.61), MACE RR 1.24 (1.15–1.34), stroke RR 1.26 (1.15–1.37)
- **Periodontal disease and diabetes**: Bidirectional relationship—poorly controlled PD makes glycemic management more difficult (Sanz et al. J Clin Periodontol 2020 consensus report)
- **Periodontal disease and Alzheimer's** (Dominy et al. Sci Adv 2019, PMID 30746447): P. gingivalis and its gingipain virulence factors found in AD patient brain tissue, correlated with tau pathology
- **Toothbrushing and chronic disease** (2025 BMC Oral Health scoping review, PMID 40484932): 21-study review—low toothbrushing frequency associated with increased risk of cardiovascular events, stroke, hypertension, metabolic syndrome, and CKD

### ⚠️ Counter-Evidence and Limitations
- **No causal RCT evidence** (AHA 2025 Scientific Statement, Circulation): Observational studies consistently show PD-ASCVD associations, but "robust RCT evidence confirming that periodontal treatment reduces CVD events is lacking." Correlation ≠ causation.
- **Healthy user bias**: Individuals who brush frequently also tend to have healthier lifestyles (diet, exercise, checkups). Studies may overestimate the independent protective effect of oral hygiene.
- **NHANES IPWRA analysis** (2024, PMC11018359): After doubly robust survival estimation adjusting for selection bias, the periodontitis–all-cause mortality association became non-significant (edentulism remained significant).
- **Self-reported data**: Most studies rely on self-reported brushing frequency rather than objective measurement.
- **Population limitations**: Evidence primarily from high-income countries (Scotland, US NHANES). Applicability to low-income settings with different oral health infrastructure is uncertain.

## Do
1. Brush twice daily, 2 minutes each time (fluoride toothpaste)
2. Floss or use interdental brushes daily—interdental cleaning is critical for periodontal health
3. Professional dental cleaning 1–2 times per year (especially for periodontal disease patients)
4. Bleeding gums signal periodontitis, not "brushing too hard"—bleeding areas need more cleaning
5. Electric toothbrushes are superior to manual in plaque removal (but habit consistency matters more than the tool)
6. Oral hygiene's value is well-documented, but it should not be seen as a substitute for other health behaviors (exercise, diet, smoking cessation)
