# Vitamin D Deficiency

**Mortality Impact**: No significant effect on all-cause mortality (RR 0.98, 95% CI 0.95–1.02), but reduces cancer mortality by 15% (RR 0.85, 95% CI 0.74–0.97)
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Large RCT meta-analysis + large RCT)
**Actionability**: High

## Conclusion
High-dose vitamin D supplementation does not reduce all-cause mortality but may lower cancer mortality by 15%. It remains important for bone health and calcium absorption; those who are deficient should supplement. D3 is superior to D2.

## Evidence
- **All-cause mortality**: BMJ 2019 meta-analysis (50 RCTs, 74,655 participants) — vitamin D supplementation had no significant effect on all-cause mortality (RR 0.98, 95% CI 0.95–1.02). However, D3 subgroup showed borderline significance (RR 0.95, 0.91–1.00, P for interaction=0.04) [PMID: 31405892]
- **Cancer mortality**: Same meta-analysis — vitamin D supplementation reduced cancer mortality risk by 15% (RR 0.85, 95% CI 0.74–0.97), with benefit observed only in the D3 group
- **Fracture risk**: VITAL trial (25,871 participants, median follow-up 5.3 years) — daily 2000 IU D3 did not significantly reduce overall fracture risk (HR 0.98), suggesting supplementation is unnecessary in non-deficient populations [PMID: 35939579]
- Vitamin D deficiency is clearly associated with osteoporosis and increased fracture risk, especially in deficient populations (elderly, institutionalized individuals) where supplementation may be beneficial
- **Vitamin D status in China**: Severe deficiency (<25 nmol/L) ~20.7%, insufficiency (25–50 nmol/L) ~63.2%; approximately 84% of the Chinese population has suboptimal vitamin D levels (eClinicalMedicine/Lancet 2021)
- **D3 vs D2**: D3 is more effective than D2 at raising serum 25(OH)D; the BMJ meta-analysis found mortality benefit trends only with D3

## Do
1. Get 15–20 minutes of gentle sun exposure daily (avoid 10:00–14:00 UV peak in summer)
2. Those who are deficient (25-hydroxyvitamin D <20 ng/mL or <50 nmol/L) should supplement with D3, 800–2000 IU/day
3. Dietary sources: Fatty fish (salmon, sardines), egg yolks, fortified milk, UV-exposed mushrooms
4. In northern regions during winter with limited sun exposure, actively test serum 25(OH)D levels

## Don't
1. Don't expect vitamin D to reduce all-cause mortality — current RCT evidence does not support it
2. Don't overdose (>4000 IU/day) — may cause hypercalcemia; combined high-dose calcium supplementation may increase kidney stone risk
3. Don't treat vitamin D as a universal supplement — insufficient evidence for cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and other indications

## Medical Red Flags
- Bone pain, recurrent fractures
- 25-hydroxyvitamin D <20 ng/mL (or <50 nmol/L)
- Persistent deficiency despite supplementation (rule out malabsorption disorders: celiac disease, Crohn's disease, etc.)
- Symptoms of hypercalcemia: nausea, vomiting, excessive thirst/urination, constipation
