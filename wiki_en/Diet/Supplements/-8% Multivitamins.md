# Multivitamins

**Mortality Impact**: Reduces cancer risk by ~-8%; no significant impact on all-cause mortality
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐ (RCT data, limited effect)
**Actionability**: Very High

## Conclusion
Multivitamins cannot extend lifespan, but may slightly reduce cancer risk. Not recommended as a "longevity supplement," but a reasonable insurance measure for those with unbalanced nutrition.

## Evidence
- COSMOS trial (21,442 participants, 3.5-year RCT): Multivitamin group had -8% cancer risk; no significant difference in cardiovascular events
- PHS-II trial (14,641 male physicians, 11-year RCT): Multivitamin group had -8% cancer risk, but no difference in total mortality
- COCOA study: Multivitamin group had -7% total mortality, but confidence interval crossed 1 (not significant)
- Key distinction: Multivitamins ≠ specific nutrients like vitamin D, Omega-3 (which have independent evidence)
- Don't expect multivitamins to replace a healthy diet—the synergistic effects of nutrients in food cannot be replicated by pills

## Do
1. Those with a balanced diet don't need multivitamins
2. The following groups may consider supplementation: elderly, pregnant women, strict vegans, those with irregular diets
3. A basic formula containing folate, B12, and D3 is sufficient; no need for "premium" formulas
4. Don't overdose—fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) have side effects in excess
5. Prioritize spending money on quality ingredients rather than supplements

## References

- Gaziano JM et al. Effect of multivitamin supplementation on cardiovascular events and cancer. JAMA. 2012.
