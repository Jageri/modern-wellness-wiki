# Multivitamins

**Mortality Impact**: No significant impact on all-cause mortality; cancer risk evidence is inconsistent (PHS-II significant -8%, COSMOS not significant)
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐ (Two large RCTs, weak and inconsistent effects)
**Actionability**: Very High

## Conclusion
Multivitamins cannot extend lifespan. PHS-II (male physicians only) showed a small ~8% reduction in cancer risk (P=0.04), but the larger COSMOS trial (both sexes) failed to replicate this finding (P=0.57). A 2024 cohort study of 390,124 participants further confirmed that daily multivitamin use is not associated with all-cause mortality. Not recommended as a "longevity supplement," but a reasonable insurance measure for those with unbalanced nutrition.

## Evidence
- **PHS-II trial** (14,641 male physicians, 11.2-year RCT, JAMA 2012): Multivitamin group had total cancer incidence of 17.0 vs 18.3/1000 person-years, HR 0.92 (95% CI 0.86-0.998, P=0.04), a modest significant ~8% reduction; cardiovascular events HR 1.01 (95% CI 0.91-1.10, P=0.91), no difference; total mortality HR 0.94 (95% CI 0.88-1.02), no significant difference
- **COSMOS trial** (21,442 participants, 3.6-year RCT, AJCN 2022): Multivitamin group had invasive cancer HR 0.97 (95% CI 0.86-1.09, P=0.57), **not significant**; cardiovascular events HR 0.98 (95% CI 0.86-1.12), not significant; all-cause mortality HR 0.93 (95% CI 0.81-1.08), not significant
- **2024 cohort study** (Loftfield et al., 390,124 participants, JAMA Netw Open): Daily multivitamin use not associated with all-cause mortality (HR 1.04, 95% CI 1.02-1.07)
- **USPSTF 2022 statement**: Current evidence is insufficient to assess the balance of benefits and harms of multivitamin supplements for CVD or cancer prevention
- Key distinction: Multivitamins ≠ specific nutrients like vitamin D, Omega-3 (which have independent evidence)
- Don't expect multivitamins to replace a healthy diet—the synergistic effects of nutrients in food cannot be replicated by pills

## Do
1. Those with a balanced diet don't need multivitamins
2. The following groups may consider supplementation: elderly, pregnant women, strict vegans, those with irregular diets
3. A basic formula containing folate, B12, and D3 is sufficient; no need for "premium" formulas
4. Don't overdose—fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) have side effects in excess
5. Prioritize spending money on quality ingredients rather than supplements
