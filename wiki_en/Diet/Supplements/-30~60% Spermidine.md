# Spermidine

**Mortality Impact**: High-intake groups show ~30–60% reduction in all-cause mortality
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐ (Strong observational studies, clear mechanisms, insufficient human RCTs)
**Actionability**: High (abundant dietary sources)

## Conclusion
Spermidine is a trending molecule in anti-aging research, acting through activation of cellular autophagy. No need to buy supplements—many common foods are naturally rich in spermidine.

## Evidence
- Austrian Bruneck cohort study (829 participants, 20-year follow-up): All-cause mortality decreased from 40.5 to 15.1 per 1000 person-years (highest vs lowest tertile, ~63% rate reduction); HR per 1-SD higher intake = 0.74 (95% CI: 0.66–0.83)
- Animal studies: Spermidine supplementation extends lifespan in yeast, nematodes, fruit flies, and mice (magnitude varies by species and experimental conditions)
- 2018 Science review: Spermidine has anti-aging, anti-cancer, cardiovascular and neuroprotective effects, and improves obesity and type 2 diabetes
- Core mechanism: Induces autophagy (cellular "deep cleaning") → clears damaged proteins and organelles → reduces oxidative stress
- Dietary sources: Soy (fermented products highest), whole wheat, mushrooms, cheese, peas, green peppers
- Human data quality: Primarily observational studies; large RCTs lacking

## Do
1. Prioritize increasing spermidine intake through diet
2. Spermidine-rich foods: natto, miso, whole wheat bread, mushrooms, aged cheese
3. Natto is one of the foods with the highest spermidine content (a possible factor in Japanese longevity)
4. Not recommended to purchase spermidine supplements (dosage unclear, insufficient long-term safety data)
