# Fish Oil / Omega-3

**Mortality Impact**: Insufficient evidence of benefit in healthy populations; may be beneficial for those with existing cardiovascular disease
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐ (Discussed by population subgroup)
**Actionability**: High

## Conclusion
Eating deep-sea fish twice a week is better than taking fish oil capsules. Those with existing cardiovascular disease should supplement as advised by their doctor. There is no evidence of mortality benefit from fish oil capsules in healthy populations.

## Evidence
- REDUCE-IT: High-purity EPA (4g/day) reduced cardiovascular events by 25% (high-risk population)
  - **Note**: This trial used pharmaceutical-grade purified EPA (Vascepa/icosapent ethyl), not ordinary fish oil capsules
  - **Conflict of interest**: Trial funded by pharmaceutical company Amarin; independent verification needed
  - **Risk signal**: High-dose EPA associated with increased atrial fibrillation risk (multiple studies suggest this)
- Multiple meta-analyses: Ordinary fish oil supplements show no significant mortality benefit in healthy populations
- Food-derived Omega-3 (fish) is more effective than supplements—possibly due to simultaneous intake of other nutrients
- Chinese residents generally have insufficient deep-sea fish intake

## Do
1. Eat deep-sea fish twice a week (salmon, mackerel, sardines)
2. Those with existing cardiovascular disease should consult their doctor about whether high-purity EPA is needed (note: it's a medication, not a supplement)
3. Those who don't eat fish can consider algae-derived DHA

## Don't
1. Don't treat fish oil as a cure-all supplement—insufficient evidence of benefit in healthy populations
2. Don't buy cheap fish oil—EPA/DHA content may be very low
3. Don't assume taking fish oil excuses an unhealthy diet
4. Don't confuse pharmaceutical-grade EPA with ordinary fish oil—they're completely different

## References
- Bhatt DL et al. Cardiovascular Risk Reduction with Icosapent Ethyl for Hypertriglyceridemia (REDUCE-IT). N Engl J Med. 2019.
- Aung T et al. Associations of Omega-3 Fatty Acid Supplement Use With Cardiovascular Disease Risks. JAMA Cardiol. 2018.
