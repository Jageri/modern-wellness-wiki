# Coffee

**Mortality Impact**: 3–4 cups per day associated with ~17% lower all-cause mortality
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Numerous prospective cohort studies + BMJ umbrella review)
**Actionability**: High

## Conclusion
3–4 cups of coffee per day is associated with reduced all-cause mortality, cardiovascular disease, and multiple cancer risks. Liver protection has the strongest evidence. Pregnant women and women at high risk of fracture should limit or avoid.

## Evidence
- BMJ umbrella review (201 meta-analyses): 3–4 cups/day associated with 17% lower all-cause mortality (RR 0.83, 95% CI 0.79–0.88)[^1]
- Liver cancer: each additional 2 cups/day reduces HCC risk by 35% (RR 0.65, 95% CI 0.59–0.72), approximately 15% per cup[^2]
- Cirrhosis: each additional 2 cups/day reduces cirrhosis risk by 44% (RR 0.56, 95% CI 0.44–0.68)[^3]
- Type 2 diabetes: highest vs lowest coffee intake, 29% lower risk (RR 0.71, 95% CI 0.67–0.76)[^4]
- Parkinson's disease: caffeine intake associated with 25% lower risk (RR 0.75, 95% CI 0.68–0.82)[^5]
- Note: All above are observational studies, not RCTs; causal relationships not established

## Do
1. 3–4 cups of black or lightly sweetened coffee daily (dose with largest benefit)
2. Finish by 3 PM (caffeine half-life is ~3–7 hours, average ~5 hours; large individual variation)[^6]
3. Choose paper-filtered coffee (paper filters effectively trap cafestol, more cholesterol-friendly)[^7]

## Don't
1. Don't add lots of sugar and cream—calories negate health benefits
2. Don't drink after 3 PM—affects sleep
3. Don't use coffee as a substitute for sleep
4. Pregnant women should limit caffeine intake (≤200 mg/day, ~1–2 cups)—high caffeine intake associated with low birth weight, preterm birth, and pregnancy loss[^1]
5. Women at high risk of osteoporosis should be cautious—coffee associated with higher fracture risk in women[^1]

## Medical Red Flags
- Persistent heart palpitations or severe insomnia after coffee
- Worsened acid reflux after coffee
