# Raw Cocoa Powder

**Mortality Impact**: Daily intake associated with ~10% lower all-cause mortality (observational); COSMOS RCT found cocoa flavanol supplementation reduced CVD death by 27%
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐ (Observational studies + RCT intermediate endpoints are robust; direct mortality RCT limited to COSMOS alone)
**Actionability**: High

## Conclusion
Raw cocoa powder is rich in flavanols and ranks among the most antioxidant-dense dietary sources known. Observational evidence links daily intake with reduced mortality, and RCTs show improvements in blood pressure and endothelial function. The core principle: **raw cocoa ≠ chocolate** — processing (alkalization, added sugar, milk) destroys large amounts of flavanols.

## Evidence
- **Mortality**: Women's Health Initiative (84,709 women, mean 19-year follow-up): ≥1 serving/day of chocolate associated with ~10% lower all-cause mortality (HR 0.90, 95% CI 0.84–0.97) and ~14% lower CVD mortality (HR 0.86, 0.78–0.95) [1]
- **Dose-response meta-analysis** (21 risk estimates, 908,390 participants, 65,407 events): Each 5g/day increase in chocolate intake associated with lower CVD incidence and mortality (pooled RR <1) [2]
- **COSMOS trial** (21,442 adults, 3.6-year RCT, Am J Clin Nutr 2022): 500mg/day cocoa flavanol supplement reduced CVD death by 27% (HR 0.73, 95% CI 0.54–0.98), though the primary composite endpoint (total CVD events) was not statistically significant (HR 0.90, 0.78–1.02, P=0.11) [3]
- **Blood pressure**: Cochrane systematic review (Ried et al. 2017, 35 RCTs): Cocoa flavanols lower systolic BP by ~2 mmHg and diastolic BP by ~1 mmHg; effects are more pronounced in prehypertensive and mildly hypertensive individuals [4]
- **Endothelial function**: Cocoa flavanols promote vasodilation by increasing endothelial nitric oxide (NO) production and improving flow-mediated dilation (FMD). EFSA recommends ≥200mg cocoa flavanols/day for this benefit
- **Core mechanism**: Flavanols → eNOS activation → NO↑ → vasodilation; also anti-inflammatory and antiplatelet effects
- **Raw vs. processed cocoa**: Dutch alkalization destroys 60–90% of flavanols; dark chocolate (≥70% cocoa solids) retains moderate amounts; raw cocoa powder retains the highest flavanol levels (~200–500mg flavanols per 10g, varying by variety and processing)

## Do
1. Consume 10–15g raw cocoa powder daily (~200–500mg flavanols), added to milk/yogurt/oatmeal/coffee
2. Choose non-alkalized (non-Dutched) raw cocoa powder — this is critical for flavanol retention
3. If you can't tolerate raw cocoa powder's taste, opt for ≥70% dark chocolate (20–30g/day), but account for calories and sugar
4. Pair with plant milk or dairy milk — does not impair flavanol absorption
5. Daily consistency matters — flavanols have a short half-life (2–4 hours), requiring continuous intake

## Don't
1. Don't use Dutch-processed (alkalized) cocoa powder — most flavanols have been destroyed
2. Don't treat milk chocolate or white chocolate as health foods — minimal cocoa solids, primarily sugar and fat
3. Don't add large amounts of sugar — high sugar negates cocoa's cardiovascular benefits
4. Don't rely on hot cocoa mixes (sugary beverage versions) — these contain very little actual cocoa
5. Don't overconsume — cocoa contains oxalates and purines; those with kidney stones or gout should limit intake

## Medical Red Flags
- Recurrent migraines after cocoa intake (cocoa contains tyramine and histamine; sensitive individuals may be triggered)
- Diagnosed kidney stones (cocoa is high in oxalates; calcium oxalate stone formers should consult a physician)
- Caution with MAOI antidepressants (cocoa contains tyramine)

## Notes
- **Heavy metal risk**: Consumer Reports 2023 testing found concerning cadmium and lead levels in some cocoa powders and dark chocolates. Choose brands with third-party testing, or rotate brands to reduce risk
- **Observational study limitation**: Mortality data come from chocolate consumption studies (not specifically raw cocoa powder), with potential healthy-user bias (chocolate consumers may have healthier lifestyles overall). Chocolate products in these studies include milk chocolate, dark chocolate, and other forms — the independent effect of cocoa flavanols cannot be isolated
- **COSMOS limitations**: Primary endpoint not significant; CVD death reduction was a secondary analysis; trial funded by Mars Inc. (cocoa product manufacturer); high-dose flavanol supplements ≠ natural cocoa powder
- **Children**: Cocoa contains caffeine (~20–30mg per 10g raw cocoa powder); young children should limit intake
