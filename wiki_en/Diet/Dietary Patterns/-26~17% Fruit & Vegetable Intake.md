# Fruit & Vegetable Intake

**Mortality Impact**: 400–800g of fruits and vegetables daily can reduce all-cause mortality by ~13–26%
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Consistent across numerous prospective studies and meta-analyses)
**Actionability**: High

## Conclusion
At least 5 servings of fruits and vegetables per day (~400g), roughly half fruit and half vegetables. This is a common feature of virtually all longevity dietary patterns worldwide. Higher intakes (up to ~800g/day) may offer additional benefits.

## Evidence
- **Aune D et al., Int J Epidemiol 2017 (PMID 28338764)**: Dose-response meta-analysis of 95 prospective studies. Per 200g/day increment, all-cause mortality RR = 0.90 (95% CI 0.87–0.93), CVD mortality RR = 0.92 (0.90–0.95), total cancer RR = 0.97 (0.95–0.99). Benefits observed up to ~800g/day. An estimated 5.6 million premature deaths worldwide in 2013 attributable to intake below 500g/day, and 7.8 million below 800g/day.
- **Wang X et al., BMJ 2014 (PMID 25073782)**: Meta-analysis of 16 prospective cohort studies. Per additional serving/day: all-cause mortality HR = 0.95 (0.92–0.98), CVD mortality HR = 0.96 (0.92–0.99). A threshold effect was observed around 5 servings/day, after which risk did not further decrease.
- **Wang DD et al., Circulation 2021 (PMID 33641343)**: 2 US cohorts + meta-analysis of 26 cohort studies. 5 vs 2 servings/day: total mortality HR = 0.87 (0.85–0.90), CVD mortality HR = 0.88 (0.83–0.94), cancer mortality HR = 0.90 (0.86–0.95), respiratory disease mortality HR = 0.65 (0.59–0.72).
- **Bondonno NP et al., Nature Communications 2019 (PMID 31409784)**: Danish cohort of 56,048 participants, 23-year follow-up. Flavonoid intake inversely associated with all-cause, CVD, and cancer mortality. Highest vs lowest quintile: ~17% reduction in all-cause mortality. Protective effect plateaued at ~500mg/day. Stronger associations in smokers and heavy alcohol consumers.
- **Du H et al., Int J Epidemiol 2017 (PMID 28449053)**: China Kadoorie Biobank, 462,342 participants, ~7-year follow-up. Regular fresh fruit consumers (≥4 days/week) vs never/rare consumers: all-cause mortality RR = 0.73 (0.70–0.76), a 27% reduction.
- Key nutrients: Dietary fiber, potassium, folate, vitamin C, polyphenols (flavonoids, etc.), carotenoids
- Vegetables ≠ potatoes: Starchy vegetables show weaker or non-significant protective effects (Aune 2017)

## Limitations & Caveats
- All evidence is from observational studies (prospective cohorts); residual confounding (healthy user bias) cannot be fully excluded
- Protective effect on cancer mortality is weaker than for CVD and non-significant in some meta-analyses
- Antioxidant supplement RCTs have not replicated the protective effects of whole fruits and vegetables (e.g., beta-carotene supplements increased lung cancer risk in smokers), suggesting synergistic effects of multiple whole-food components
- Potatoes and other starchy vegetables should not be treated as equivalent to leafy greens and cruciferous vegetables

## Do
1. At least 5 servings daily: 2 fruit + 3 vegetables (1 serving ≈ 80g); can gradually increase to 800g/day
2. The more colorful, the better: dark leafy greens, red tomatoes, orange carrots, purple eggplant
3. Prioritize local, seasonal produce (higher nutrient density)
4. Juice ≠ fruit (juice removes fiber and concentrates sugar)
5. Daily cup of tea + 1 apple + 100g blueberries + 100g broccoli = 500mg+ flavonoids (plateau-level dose)
