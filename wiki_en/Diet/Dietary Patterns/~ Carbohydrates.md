# Carbohydrates

**Mortality Impact**: Both extreme low-carb (<20%) and extreme high-carb (>70%) increase mortality; ~50% is optimal
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Consistent across multiple large meta-analyses, but optimal ratio remains debated)
**Actionability**: High

## Conclusion
Carbohydrates are one of the most controversial topics in nutrition. Both the keto and high-carb camps have "evidence" to support them. The core consensus: **carbohydrate quality matters far more than quantity**; the U-shaped curve shows all-cause mortality is lowest at ~50% carbohydrate energy.

## Evidence
- Lancet Public Health meta-analysis (15,428 participants, 25-year follow-up): 50–55% carbohydrate → lowest mortality; <40% → 4 years reduced life expectancy; >70% → 1 year reduced life expectancy
- BMJ study: Optimal energy source ratio is <24% starch, 15–17% protein, >15% monounsaturated fat, <15% sugar
- Blue Zones: The world's longest-lived populationspredominantly highcarb (primarily whole grains and legumes); Okinawan carb energy is ~85%

## Both Sides of the Debate

**Low-carb (<20–30%) evidence:**
- Significant short-term (<2 years) weight loss
- Improved type 2 diabetes blood glucose control
- Lower triglycerides, higher HDL
- Virta Health trial: 60+ diabetes remission rate on ketogenic diet

**High-carb (50–60%) evidence:**
- Lancet data: 50–55% → lowest mortality
- Longevity regions (Okinawa, Sardinia) arepredominantly highcarb
- High-intensity exercise depends on carbs for fuel
- Dietary fiber (a type of carb) is the core fuel for gut microbiota

**Consensus:**
- Carb quality > carb quantity
- Whole grains, legumes, vegetables, fruits (good carbs) vs refined grains, sugar (bad carbs)
- ~50% is the range with lowest long-term mortality
- Extreme low-carb (<20%) may increase cardiovascular risk long-term (LDL elevation)

## Do
1. 40–55% carbohydrate energy is the safe range; don't pursue extreme low-carb
2. Choose low-GI carbs: brown rice, oats, whole wheat bread, sweet potatoes, legumes
3. Reduce refined carbs: white rice, white bread, noodles (especially in large quantities)
4. Avoid liquid sugar: sugary drinks, juice (extremely fast absorption)
5. Individual variation is large: Insulin-sensitive individuals can tolerate higher carbs; insulin-resistant individuals may need less
6. If doing low-carb/keto: Recommended under medical supervision with regular lipid monitoring
7. Protein and fat sources are equally important—it's not simply about reducing carbs and adding fat
