# Ultra-Processed Foods

**Mortality Impact**: All-cause mortality +15–21% (highest vs lowest intake groups; results vary across meta-analyses)
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Consistent across multiple meta-analyses)
**Actionability**: High
**⚠️ Note**: All evidence is observational; residual confounding (smoking, socioeconomic status) may influence results.

## Conclusion
Eat less. If you can avoid them, do. The shorter the ingredient list, the better.

## Evidence
- Latest meta-analysis (18 studies, 1,148,387 participants): Highest UPF intake group had +15% all-cause mortality risk (HR 1.15, 95% CI 1.09–1.22) (Liang 2025, PMID: 40033461)
- Each 10% increase in UPF intake → ~10% increase in mortality risk (HR 1.10, 95% CI 1.04–1.16) (Liang 2025)
- BMJ umbrella review: UPF associated with +21% all-cause mortality (RR 1.21, 95% CI 1.15–1.27) (Lane 2024, PMID: 38418082)
- Another meta-analysis: highest intake group +29% mortality risk (RR 1.29, 95% CI 1.17–1.42) (Taneri 2022, PMID: 35231930)
- Primary mechanisms: Additives alter gut microbiota; high sugar/salt/fat triggers chronic inflammation; high calorie density leads to overconsumption. However, smoking pack-years strongly confound the association—adjusted HRs attenuated toward null (Fang 2024, BMJ, PMID: 38719536)
- Ultra-processed food definition (NOVA group 4): Industrial formulations made from substances extracted from foods or synthesized from food constituents, typically containing sugar, oils, fats, salt, and cosmetic additives (emulsifiers, colorants, flavorings, thickeners, etc.), such as instant noodles, sausages, chips, sugary drinks, packaged cakes

## Do
1. Check ingredient lists—be cautious about processed foods with 5+ ingredients
2. Replace with whole foods: fruit instead of juice, rice instead of bread, home-cooked meals instead of takeout
3. Occasional consumption is fine, but don't make them a daily staple
4. Shop the supermarket perimeter (fresh produce section); avoid central aisles (processed food section)

## Don't
1. Don't be misled by "low-fat," "high-fiber," or "0 sugar" labels—health labels on ultra-processed foods ≠ healthy
2. Don't assume "imported is better"—the issue with ultra-processed foods is the processing method, not the origin
3. Don't overlook hidden ultra-processed foods: flavored yogurt, breakfast cereals, plant-based meat
