# Caloric Restriction

**Mortality Impact**: 2.9× reduction in age-related mortality (HR 2.89) in the Wisconsin rhesus monkey study; human data limited to observational studies and short-term RCT biomarker improvements
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐½ (Strong causal evidence from animal studies, but conflicting results between studies; human evidence limited to CALERIE trial biomarkers over 2 years)
**Actionability**: Medium (requires sustained willpower; 25% restriction was achievable over 2 years in CALERIE)

## Conclusion
Eating less is one of the most evidence-supported life-extension interventions, though conclusions come primarily from animal studies. Of two large rhesus monkey studies, the University of Wisconsin found a significant life-extension effect, while the NIA study did not find the same conclusion (possibly due to differences in actual control group food intake). The human CALERIE trial showed that 2 years of 25% caloric restriction significantly improves cardiometabolic markers. No need for extreme dieting—"eating until 80% full" already provides significant benefits.

## Evidence
- **University of Wisconsin rhesus monkey study (Colman et al. 2009/2014)**: 30% caloric restriction; age-related mortality HR 2.89 (95% CI: 1.34–6.25, P=0.007), all-cause mortality HR 1.78 (95% CI: 1.04–3.04, P=0.037)
- **NIA rhesus monkey study (Mattison et al. 2012)**: Did not find improved survival with caloric restriction, conflicting with Wisconsin findings
- **Joint analysis (Mattison et al. 2017)**: Differences between the two studies may relate to actual control group food intake, study design, and CR onset age; joint analysis confirmed that CR health benefits are conserved in primates
- **Okinawan "Hara Hachi Bu" (eat until 80% full)**: Willcox et al. (2007) epidemiological analysis linked low caloric intake in Okinawan elders to extended mean and maximum lifespan
- **CALERIE-II human RCT (Ravussin et al. 2015; Kraus et al. 2019)**: 25% caloric restriction over 2 years (actual achievement ~11.9%) → comprehensive improvements in cardiometabolic markers (blood pressure, lipids, insulin sensitivity, CRP)
- **Mechanisms**: Reduces mTOR pathway activity, decreases oxidative stress, improves insulin sensitivity, activates autophagy
- **Intermittent fasting (16:8 or 5:2)**: Partially mimics caloric restriction effects; easier to sustain (de Cabo et al. 2019, NEJM)
- **Controversy**: Long-term extreme caloric restriction (<1200 kcal/day) may cause muscle loss and decreased bone density; however, the CALERIE trial found no clinically significant bone density decline over 2 years of 25% CR, and combining with strength training effectively prevents muscle loss

## Do
1. **80% full rule**: Eat until no longer hungry but not full; slow down your eating pace
2. **Intermittent fasting**: 16:8 (8-hour daily eating window) or 5:2 (2 low-calorie days per week)
3. **Smallest dinner**: Blue Zones pattern—largest breakfast, smallest dinner
4. **Not recommended**: Long-term extreme caloric restriction (<1200 kcal/day); may cause malnutrition
5. **Combine with strength training**: Prevent muscle loss from caloric restriction
