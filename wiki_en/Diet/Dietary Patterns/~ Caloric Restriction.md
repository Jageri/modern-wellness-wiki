# Caloric Restriction

**Mortality Impact**: Reduces mortality by 60–70% in animal studies; human data incomplete but consistently points toward benefit
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Causal evidence in animal studies; human data is observational)
**Actionability**: Medium (requires sustained willpower)

## Conclusion
Eating less is one of the most evidence-supported life-extension interventions currently available. No need for extreme dieting—"eating until 80% full" already provides significant benefits. Intermittent fasting is an easier-to-execute alternative.

## Evidence
- Rhesus monkey experiment (University of Wisconsin, 20 years): 30% caloric restriction → 2.9× lower disease risk, 3.0× lower mortality risk
- Okinawan "Hara Hachi Bu" (eat until 80% full): Common habit among Okinawan centenarians
- CALERIE trial (human RCT): 25% caloric restriction for 2 years → comprehensive improvement in cardiometabolic markers
- Mechanisms: Reduces mTOR pathway activity, decreases oxidative stress, improves insulin sensitivity, activates autophagy
- Intermittent fasting (16:8 or 5:2): Partially mimics caloric restriction effects; easier to sustain
- Controversy: Long-term extreme caloric restriction may cause muscle loss, decreased bone density, and reduced immunity

## Do
1. **80% full rule**: Eat until no longer hungry but not full; slow down your eating pace
2. **Intermittent fasting**: 16:8 (8-hour daily eating window) or 5:2 (2 low-calorie days per week)
3. **Smallest dinner**: Blue Zones pattern—largest breakfast, smallest dinner
4. **Not recommended**: Long-term extreme caloric restriction (<1200 kcal/day); may cause malnutrition
5. **Combine with strength training**: Prevent muscle loss from caloric restriction
