# Excessive Red Meat

**Mortality Impact**: Each half-serving (~42g cooked weight) daily increase in red meat → all-cause mortality +10% (8-year follow-up); each 1 serving/day increase → all-cause mortality +12% (pooled analysis)
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐ (Prospective cohort studies; weaker evidence than processed meat)
**Actionability**: High

## Conclusion
No more than 500g red meat per week (cooked weight). Prioritize poultry and fish.

## Evidence
- Zheng 2019 BMJ cohort study: An increase of half a serving (~42g) of red meat per day over 8 years was associated with a 10% higher all-cause mortality in the subsequent 8 years (HR 1.10, 95% CI 1.04–1.17); 12-year subgroup analysis showed +12% (HR 1.12, 95% CI 1.03–1.22)
- Pan 2012 JAMA Intern Med pooled analysis: Each 1 serving/day increase in red meat → all-cause mortality +12% (HR 1.12, 95% CI 1.09–1.15)
- Red meat is associated with colorectal cancer, cardiovascular disease, and type 2 diabetes risk (IARC 2015 evaluation)
- Mechanisms: Heme iron overload, TMAO (gut microbiome metabolite of carnitine/choline), carcinogens produced by high-temperature cooking (heterocyclic amines, polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons)
- White meat (poultry, fish) does not show similar risk
- WHO/IARC classification: Processed meat is a Group 1 carcinogen (carcinogenic to humans); red meat is Group 2A (probably carcinogenic to humans)
- ⚠️ Controversy: The 2019 NutriRECS guidelines rated evidence certainty as low and suggested maintaining current red meat intake, but were widely criticized by Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health and others

## Do
1. Partially replace red meat with poultry, fish, and legumes
2. Limit red meat to 300–500g per week (cooked weight)
3. Low-temperature cooking is preferable to high-temperature frying

## Don't
1. Don't eliminate red meat entirely—it's a good source of iron and B12
2. Don't eat processed meat (sausages, bacon are more dangerous than red meat)
3. Don't use high-temperature frying—produces more carcinogens
