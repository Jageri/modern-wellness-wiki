# Ultra-Processed Foods

**Mortality Impact**: All-cause mortality +15–30% (highest vs lowest intake groups; results vary across meta-analyses)
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Consistent across multiple meta-analyses)
**Actionability**: High

## Conclusion
Eat less. If you can avoid them, do. The shorter the ingredient list, the better.

## Evidence
- BMJ meta-analysis (~10 million participants): Highest ultra-processed food intake group had +30% all-cause mortality risk (Rico-Campà 2019)
- BMJ systematic review (98 studies): Ultra-processed food associated with +15–21% all-cause mortality (Lane 2024)
- Each 10% increase in ultra-processed food intake → ~6% increase in mortality risk
- Primary mechanisms: Additives alter gut microbiota; high sugar/salt/fat triggers chronic inflammation; high calorie density leads to overconsumption
- Ultra-processed food definition: Contains 5+ industrial additives (emulsifiers, colorants, flavorings, thickeners, etc.), such as instant noodles, sausages, chips, sugary drinks, packaged cakes

## Do
1. Check ingredient lists—be cautious about processed foods with 5+ ingredients
2. Replace with whole foods: fruit instead of juice, rice instead of bread, home-cooked meals instead of takeout
3. Occasional consumption is fine, but don't make them a daily staple
4. Shop the supermarket perimeter (fresh produce section); avoid central aisles (processed food section)

## Don't
1. Don't be misled by "low-fat," "high-fiber," or "0 sugar" labels—health labels on ultra-processed foods ≠ healthy
2. Don't assume "imported is better"—the issue with ultra-processed foods is the processing method, not the origin
3. Don't overlook hidden ultra-processed foods: flavored yogurt, breakfast cereals, plant-based meat

## References

- Lane MM et al. Ultra-processed food exposure and adverse health outcomes. BMJ. 2024.
