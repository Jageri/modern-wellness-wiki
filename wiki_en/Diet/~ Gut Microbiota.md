# Gut Microbiota

**Mortality Impact**: No direct human mortality data, but dysbiosis is strongly associated with multiple chronic diseases
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐ (Strong correlational evidence; insufficient causal intervention evidence)
**Actionability**: Medium

## Conclusion
Gut microbiota is one of the hottest topics in health in recent years, and a classic case of "we know it's important but don't know how to intervene." The best-evidenced interventions are **eating more dietary fiber** and **eating more fermented foods**—both more reliable than any probiotic supplement. Fermented foods showed more consistent effects on increasing microbiota diversity and reducing inflammation (Wastyk 2021 RCT), while dietary fiber effects varied significantly by individual baseline microbiota.

## Evidence
- The human gut contains ~38 trillion microorganisms (Sender 2016 Cell), with 150× the gene count of humans (Qin 2010 Nature)
- Centenarians have unique gut microbiota: higher diversity than average elderly, enriched with specific butyrate-producing bacteria (Biagi 2016)
- Dysbiosis is associated with: obesity, type 2 diabetes, inflammatory bowel disease, depression, autism, Parkinson's disease, colorectal cancer
- Key mechanism: Short-chain fatty acids (especially butyrate) → maintain intestinal barrier → regulate immunity → reduce systemic inflammation
- Probiotic meta-analyses: Effects vary by individual; overall benefits are modest and inconsistent
- Fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT): ~94% cure rate for recurrent C. difficile infection (van Nood 2013 NEJM RCT), but efficacy for other diseases is unclear
- Core issue: Everyone's microbiota composition is different; a "one-size-fits-all" probiotic approach may be ineffective

## Do
1. **Eat more fermented foods** (most consistent evidence): Yogurt, kefir, kimchi, natto, miso—natural probiotic sources
   - Wastyk 2021 RCT found: high-fermented-food diet steadily increased microbiota diversity and decreased inflammatory markers
2. **Eat more dietary fiber** (30g+ daily)
   - Dietary fiber is "food" for probiotics (prebiotics), but effects vary significantly by individual baseline microbiota
   - Sources: whole grains, legumes, vegetables, fruits
3. **Reduce ultra-processed foods**: They destroy microbiota diversity
4. **Don't overuse antibiotics**: A single course can wipe outlarge amounts ofbeneficial bacteria; recovery takes months
5. **Probiotic supplements**: If trying, choose products with multiple strains; take continuously for at least 4 weeks before evaluating
6. **Don't expect probiotics to "treat disease"**—they're an adjunct, not a treatment
