# Gut Microbiota

**Mortality Impact**: No direct human mortality data, but dysbiosis is strongly associated with multiple chronic diseases
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐ (Strong correlational evidence; insufficient causal intervention evidence)
**Actionability**: Medium

## Conclusion
Gut microbiota is one of the hottest topics in health in recent years, and a classic case of "we know it's important but don't know how to intervene." The only intervention currently confirmed to be effective is **eating more dietary fiber**—more reliable than any probiotic.

## Evidence
- The human gut contains ~38 trillion microorganisms, with 150× the gene count of humans
- Centenarians have unique gut microbiota: higher diversity than average elderly, enriched with specific butyrate-producing bacteria
- Dysbiosis is associated with: obesity, type 2 diabetes, inflammatory bowel disease, depression, autism, Parkinson's disease, colorectal cancer
- Key mechanism: Short-chain fatty acids (especially butyrate) → maintain intestinal barrier → regulate immunity → reduce systemic inflammation
- Probiotic meta-analyses: Effects vary by individual; overall benefits are modest and inconsistent
- Fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT): >90% cure rate for C. difficile infection, but efficacy for other diseases is unclear
- Core issue: Everyone's microbiota composition is different; a "one-size-fits-all" probiotic approach may be ineffective

## Do
1. **Most effective intervention: Eat more dietary fiber** (30g+ daily)
   - Dietary fiber is "food" for probiotics (prebiotics), more reliable than taking probiotics directly
   - Sources: whole grains, legumes, vegetables, fruits
2. **Fermented foods**: Yogurt, kefir, kimchi, natto, miso—natural probiotic sources
3. **Reduce ultra-processed foods**: They destroy microbiota diversity
4. **Don't overuse antibiotics**: A single course can wipe outlarge amounts ofbeneficial bacteria; recovery takes months
5. **Probiotic supplements**: If trying, choose products with multiple strains; take continuously for at least 4 weeks before evaluating
6. **Don't expect probiotics to "treat disease"**—they're an adjunct, not a treatment
