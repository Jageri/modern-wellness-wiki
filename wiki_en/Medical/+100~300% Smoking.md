# Smoking

**Mortality Impact**: All-cause mortality +100–300%, average 10 years of life lost
**Evidence Level**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Causal relationship established)
**Actionability**: High (quittable)

## Conclusion
Quit. Quitting at any age provides benefits; quitting by age 30 can recover nearly all of the 10-year life expectancy loss.

## Evidence
- Smokers die about 10 years younger than non-smokers; quitting recovers: age 60→+3 years, 50→+6, 40→+9, 30→+10 [1]
- Smokers have 2–3× the all-cause mortality of non-smokers [2]
- Lung cancer risk: 15–30× that of non-smokers [2]
- Cardiovascular disease risk: ~2–3× [2]
- COPD risk: ~12–13× [2]
- **Note**: Causal relationship established (2014 U.S. Surgeon General report)

## Do
1. Any form of smoking cessation aid is worth trying (nicotine replacement, medication, behavioral therapy)
2. E-cigarettes may be considered as a transitional tool, but are not the final solution
3. Heart rate begins recovering within 20 minutes of quitting; cardiovascular risk halves within 1 year
4. Avoid secondhand smoke exposure (passive smoking increases lung cancer risk by ~21% in non-smokers) [3]

## Don't
1. Don't think "I've smoked for so many years, quitting won't help"—quitting at any age provides benefits
2. Don't comfort yourself with "a little is harmless"—there is no safe level of smoking
3. Don't substitute reducing intake for quitting—cutting down doesn't proportionally reduce risk

## Medical Red Flags
- Persistent cough lasting >3 weeks
- Blood in sputum
- Unexplained weight loss
- Annual recommendation: Low-dose CT screening (age 50+, 30 pack-year smoking history)
